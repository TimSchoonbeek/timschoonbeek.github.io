Supervised Representation Learning Approach towards Assembly State Verification Generalizing to New Part Configurations

Tim J. Schoonbeek1, Goutham Balachandran1, Hans Onvlee2, Jacek Kustra2, Tim Houben1, Peter H.N. de With1, Fons van der Sommen1,
1Eindhoven University of Technology, 2ASML Research

Published in: CVPR 2024 LIMIT Workshop

Paper arXiv Video Code Data

TLDR: Tackling assembly state recognition as a representation learning problem makes it scalable. We test on various error types and publish the data.

Abstract

Assembly state verification facilitates the execution of assembly procedures, offering feedback to enhance efficiency and minimize errors. However, recognizing assembly states poses challenges in scalability, since parts are frequently updated or added. Moreover, the robustness of such algorithms to erroneous states remains largely underexplored. To address these challenges, this paper introduces SimStateNet, an approach for assembly state verification based on representation learning.

SimStateNet compares real-world assembly states with synthetic anchor images derived from CAD data, achieving high precision without the need for explicit domain adaptation techniques. Notably, despite being trained exclusively on correctly assembled states, thorough analysis on error states demonstrates that various execution errors in real-world assembly procedures are correctly identified as erroneous. The integration of SimStateNet has the potential to significantly improve efficiency and reduce errors in industrial settings, offering meaningful assistance to workers and mitigating unexpected losses due to procedural mishaps. For further details and implementation, the code is publicly available.

Overview of the work

SimStateNet framework

In training, synthetic images are passed through a fully convolutional encoder, followed by a three-layer MLP projection head. The resulting embeddings are used to calculate the contrastive loss. During inference, only the first layer of the projection head is used. In this stage, a synthetic image of an assembly state is passed through the network, together with a real-world test image. A similarity function sim() is applied on the resulting embeddings to determine whether the two images represent the same assembly states.

Dataset

We extend upon the IndustReal dataset by providing extra labels: clean, intended state, and error category. Error category is based on a four error types frequently occuring in the dataset: missing component (I), incorrect orientation (II), incorrect placement (III), and part-level errors (IV). The intended state represent the assembly state intended by the operator. Finally, the label 'clean' indicates whether (1) the entire assembly is contained within the image, (2) the motion blur does not hinder human recognition of the assembly state, and (3) the bounding box is reasonably placed around the assembly state.

Results

SimStateNet's performance on assembly states without execution errors is high, in line with expectations based on previously existing approaches. Particularly, an average precision of 0.985 and area under the receiver-operator curve of 0.982 is achieved. Additionally, contrastive losses outperform cross-entropy based approaches even on classification metrics by 8%.

Qualitative examples showing the SimStateNet performance trained with the InfoNCE loss on 2 correct and 6 error state images from all 4 categories. SimStateNet performs well in verifying component-level errors, demonstrated by a lower similarity between the (correct) anchor and error state compared to the similarity with a correct real-world sample, except for some hard cases in placement errors. Contrarily, for most part-level errors, there is a high degree of similarity between the anchor and incorrect states.
